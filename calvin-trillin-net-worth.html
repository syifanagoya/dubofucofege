Calvin Trillin Overview and Wiki

Early Journalism and The New Yorker

Calvin Trillin‘s career in journalism began after his service in the U.S. Army. He started as a reporter for Time magazine, a role that laid the foundation for his future endeavors in writing and journalism.

In 1963, Trillin joined the staff of The New Yorker, marking the beginning of a long and fruitful association with the magazine. His work for The New Yorker was significant, particularly his U.S. Journal series, which he wrote from 1967 to 1982.

This series covered a wide range of local events across the United States, both serious and quirky, showcasing his unique perspective and narrative style.

Literary Contributions and Food Writing

Trillin’s career is not limited to journalism; he is also an acclaimed author, particularly known for his food writing. His love for food is evident in his books like American Fried (1974), Alice, Let’s Eat (1978), and Third Helpings (1983).

These books were later compiled into a compendium titled The Tummy Trilogy in 1994. His writing often blends humor with culinary experiences, making his work appealing to a broad audience.

Autobiographical Works and Contributions to The Nation

In addition to food writing, Trillin has penned several autobiographical books and magazine articles. His works such as Messages from My Father (1996) and Family Man (1998) offer insights into his personal life and experiences.

Trillin has also been a contributor to The Nation since 1978, starting with a column called Variations, later renamed Uncivil Liberties.

His columns often included humor and satire, and he has been known for his humorous poems about current events in his Deadline Poet column in The Nation.

Novels and Short Stories

Trillin’s literary talents extend to fiction as well. He has written a collection of short stories titled Barnett Frummer is an Unbloomed Flower (1969) and comic novels such as Runestruck (1977), Floater (1980), and Tepper Isn’t Going Out (2002).

His novel Tepper Isn’t Going Out is particularly notable for its unique exploration of the subject of parking in New York City.

Awards and Recognition

Throughout his career, Trillin has received numerous accolades. He was awarded the Thurber Prize for American Humor in 2012 for Quite Enough of Calvin Trillin: Forty Years of Funny Stuff.

Additionally, he was elected to the American Academy of Arts and Letters in 2008, further cementing his status as a significant figure in American literature and journalism.

Personal Life and Relationships

Trillin’s personal life has been marked by his marriage to Alice Stewart Trillin, who was not just his wife but also a muse for some of his writings. The couple’s life in Greenwich Village, New York City, and their two daughters have been central themes in his autobiographical narratives.
